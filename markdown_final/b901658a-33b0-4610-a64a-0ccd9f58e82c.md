## Fluid resuscitation protocols in the WATERFALL trial

The WATERFALL trial compared aggressive versus moderate fluid resuscitation in acute pancreatitis, with protocols explicitly based on **actual body weight**. Aggressive fluid resuscitation used a **20 mL/kg bolus** of lactated Ringer's within 2 hours, followed by **3 mL/kg/h** for 12 hours and then **1.5 mL/kg/h** to 24 hours [^1119KAhp]. Moderate fluid resuscitation used a **10 mL/kg bolus** if hypovolemic or **no bolus** if normovolemic, followed by **1.5 mL/kg/h** for 24 hours. Both arms used **actual body weight** for all calculations [^1119KAhp].

## Rationale for using actual body weight

The trial's use of **actual body weight** reflects standard practice in acute pancreatitis, where fluid needs are typically indexed to actual weight. The protocol explicitly states volumes per kilogram of actual body weight, with no mention of ideal or adjusted weight.

## Clinical implications of using actual body weight

Using **actual body weight** can increase fluid volumes in overweight or obese patients, potentially raising the risk of fluid overload [^111sFbig]. In WATERFALL, aggressive resuscitation (using **actual body weight**) led to higher fluid overload rates (20.5% vs 6.3%) without reducing severe pancreatitis, supporting more moderate, individualized strategies [^1119KAhp].

### Predicted body weight relationships for protective ventilation - unisex proposals from pre-term through to adult [^113Qh8vw]. BMC Pulmonary Medicine (2017). Low credibility.

This remains common practice for pediatric ventilation and much of adult ventilation. Lung-protective ventilation has also been shown to improve outcomes in patients ventilated in the operating room and in the intensive care unit (ICU). ### Hypernatremia [^1119PAbP]. The New England Journal of Medicine (2000). High credibility.

The clinical calculator "Free Water Deficit in Hypernatremia" provides an essential tool for estimating the necessary amount of free water (in liters) to correct hypernatremia, based on a patient's sodium levels, weight, and total body water (TBW) percentage.

The calculator evaluates the free water deficit in individuals with hypernatremia by integrating five key inputs: gender, age group, weight, serum sodium level, and desired serum sodium level. This metric is invaluable for determining how much free water is necessary to adjust serum sodium levels back to normal.

The calculation process unfolds through these steps:

- **Weight conversion**: The individual's weight can be entered in either kilograms or pounds. If pounds are used, the calculator converts this to kilograms: Weight in kg = Weight in lbs / 2.20462.

- **Total body water (TBW) fraction**: This fraction represents the portion of body weight that comprises water. Its determination hinges on both the individual's gender and age group:
	- The TBW fraction is 0.6 for children, adult males, and non-elderly males.
	- For adult females, the TBW fraction adapts to 0.5.
	- In the elderly, it decreases to 0.5 for males and further to 0.45 for females.

- **Sodium levels**: Input for serum sodium levels is accepted in mEq/L or mmol/L. Given the equivalence of these units, no conversion is needed.

- **Computation of free water deficit**: This quantity is calculated with the formula: Free Water Deficit (L) = TBW fraction × Weight (kg) × [(Serum Sodium / Desired Serum Sodium) - 1].
