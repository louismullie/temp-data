Perichondritis is an **inflammation of the perichondrium** [^115fiZpZ], most often affecting the auricle and sparing the earlobe [^117V6nXF]. It is usually caused by **trauma, piercings, or surgery**, with Pseudomonas aeruginosa as the most common pathogen [^113AVCPD]. Clinically, it presents with erythema, swelling, tenderness, and sometimes purulent discharge; if untreated, it can progress to **cartilage necrosis and cauliflower ear deformity** [^111tSnhM]. Diagnosis is clinical, with imaging reserved for complications [^112ko4kV]. Management requires prompt antibiotics with antipseudomonal coverage (e.g. fluoroquinolones) [^111tSnhM], and surgical drainage or debridement if abscess or necrosis is present [^114ZzmPH], and **early treatment is essential to prevent permanent deformity** [^113AVCPD].

## Etiology and risk factors

The most common **etiologies and risk factors** include:

- **Trauma**: Blunt or penetrating trauma to the auricle, including sports injuries and accidents [^111tSnhM].

- **Piercings**: High ear cartilage piercings significantly increase the risk of perichondritis [^113AVCPD].

- **Surgical procedures**: Manipulation of auricular cartilage during surgical procedures, such as Mohs reconstructions, can lead to perichondritis [^115WsPJV].

- **Infections**: Bacterial infections, particularly by Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus, are common causative agents [^113AVCPD].

- **Systemic conditions**: Immunocompromised states, such as diabetes mellitus, increase susceptibility to perichondritis [^1156E45q].

---

## Clinical presentation

Typical **clinical features** include:

- **Erythema and swelling**: Redness and swelling of the affected cartilage, sparing the earlobe [^117V6nXF].

- **Pain and tenderness**: Significant pain and tenderness upon palpation.

- **Purulent discharge**: In severe cases, purulent discharge may be present.

- **Cartilage deformity**: If untreated, chronic inflammation can lead to cartilage necrosis and deformity, commonly known as "cauliflower ear" [^111tSnhM].

---

## Diagnostic methods

Diagnosis is **primarily clinical**, based on the history and physical examination. Imaging studies, such as ultrasound or MRI, may be used in complicated cases to assess the extent of cartilage involvement or abscess formation [^115dxUzg].

---

## Treatment strategies

Effective management involves **prompt initiation of antibiotic therapy** and surgical intervention for severe cases:

- **Antibiotic therapy**: Empirical antibiotic treatment targeting Pseudomonas aeruginosa and Staphylococcus aureus is essential [^111tSnhM]. Fluoroquinolones (e.g. ciprofloxacin) are commonly used due to their effectiveness against Pseudomonas species [^113A8jH6].

- **Surgical intervention**: In cases of abscess formation or cartilage necrosis, surgical drainage and debridement are necessary to prevent permanent deformity [^117V6nXF].

- **Supportive care**: Analgesics and anti-inflammatory medications may be used to manage pain and inflammation [^117V6nXF].

---

## Potential complications

Untreated perichondritis can lead to **cartilage necrosis**, with cartilage destruction and **permanent deformity** [^117V6nXF]. It may also cause cauliflower ear due to chronic inflammation and fibrosis [^111tSnhM]. Chronic infection can occur, necessitating prolonged antibiotic therapy and possible surgical intervention.

---

## Prevention

Prevention focuses on **minimizing auricular trauma** by using protective gear during sports and handling the auricle carefully. Safe piercing with sterile technique and diligent aftercare reduces infection risk [^116jcKsu]. Prompt treatment of auricular hematomas [^111tSnhM] and infections helps prevent progression to perichondritis.

Auricular hematoma is most commonly associated with shearing or blunt trauma to the ear, resulting in the formation of a hematoma within the subperichondrial space.
