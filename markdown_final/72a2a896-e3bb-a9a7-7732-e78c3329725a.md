Struvite stones are **always associated with infection** because they form only when urease-producing bacteria (e.g. Proteus, Klebsiella, Enterococcus) alkalinize urine and generate ammonia, creating supersaturation of magnesium ammonium phosphate [^3f4821f8] [^f79740a8]. This is a defining feature of struvite stones and distinguishes them from metabolic stones. Even if cultures are negative at presentation, prior or intermittent infection is typically identifiable on careful review or with targeted testing, so **infection is integral to their pathogenesis** [^2270283b].

---

## Pathophysiological basis linking struvite stones to infection

- **Urease-producing bacteria**: Struvite stones form when urease-producing bacteria hydrolyze urea to ammonia and CO2, raising urine pH and precipitating magnesium ammonium phosphate [^3f4821f8] [^f79740a8].

- **Alkaline urine**: pH > 7.2 favors struvite crystallization; this alkalinization is a direct consequence of bacterial urease activity [^3f4821f8].

- **Biofilm formation**: Bacteria within the stone matrix create biofilms that protect them from antibiotics and host defenses, perpetuating infection and stone growth [^3f4821f8].

---

## Clinical evidence supporting the association

- **Stone culture studies**: Stone cultures are positive in ~72% of struvite cases, with urea-splitting organisms historically predominant; even when stone cultures are negative, many patients have prior urine cultures positive for urease producers [^2270283b].

- **Guideline statements**: The AUA explicitly states struvite stones occur as a consequence of urinary infection with urease-producing organisms, reinforcing infection as a core feature [^efe7fccd].

- **Treatment implications**: Complete stone removal plus antibiotics is required to eradicate infection and prevent recurrence, underscoring the causal relationship [^9857a589] [^5c74a587].

---

## Exceptions and controversies

Although struvite stones are infection-related, **culture-negative cases** can occur due to prior antibiotics or intermittent urease activity; however, retrospective cultures or molecular methods often reveal prior or low-level infection [^2270283b]. Mixed-composition stones containing struvite may also include metabolic components, but the struvite portion still reflects infection-driven crystallization [^7b3baef5].

---

## Clinical implications and management

- **Antibiotic therapy**: Culture-directed antibiotics are essential, but stones must be removed to eradicate the nidus of infection [^9857a589] [^5c74a587].

- **Surgical management**: PCNL is the gold standard for large struvite stones; ureteroscopy or SWL may be adjuncts in select cases [^9857a589].

- **Prevention**: Long-term surveillance for reinfection and strategies to acidify urine (e.g. acetohydroxamic acid) are recommended in recurrent cases [^40454ac0] [^b380f651].

### Evidence of bacterial imprints in different types of non-struvite kidney stones [^eed58f9d]. BMC Urology (2025). Medium credibility.

Introduction

Infection stones of the urinary system account for 5 to 15% of all urinary stones, and most of them consist of struvite, also known as magnesium ammonium phosphate or triple phosphate. More precisely, with elevated urine pH due to infection of the urinary tract, the patient becomes prone to form magnesium ammonium phosphate hexahydrate (MAP or struvite), carbonated calcium phosphate apatite (carbonated apatite or CA), amorphous carbonated calcium phosphate (ACCP) or whitlockite (Wk) stones. Although the prevalence of struvite stones (which are always attributable to urinary tract infections) is lower in industrialized countries, these stones are becoming more common in these regions, possibly because of increases in resistance to antibiotics. The formation of a struvite stone begins when bacterial urease catalyzes the hydrolysis of urea into ammonia and CO 2; ammonia is then ionized into the ammonium ion, which significantly alkalizes the urine, leading to the transformation of CO 2 into carbonate. These conditions lead to a high supersaturation of struvite and calcium phosphocarbonate (apatite), causing them to crystallize into stones. Stones will grow at different rates, some slow and some fast, depending on the type of stone and urinary conditions, which may change over time (they may be different at the time of stone formation than at the time of stone removal). Recently, it was found that bacteria also play a role in the specific morphogenesis of struvite by control of nucleation and shape of the growing crystal.

A study showed that stones consisting of calcium phosphocarbonate but without struvite crystals had clear evidence of bacterial imprints. Because phosphate stones without struvite can have a high carbonation of apatite and bacterial imprints, this suggests that microorganisms with weak ureolytic activity may contribute to the formation of these stones. Furthermore, other studies found that bacterial activity was associated with stones that lacked carbonated apatite, such as those mainly composed of calcium oxalate or a mixture of oxalate and calcium phosphate.

In this paper we present a study of bacterial imprints in different types of stones that contained carbonated apatite but lacked struvite.

### Evidence of bacterial imprints in different types of non-struvite kidney stones [^498203aa]. BMC Urology (2025). Medium credibility.

Discussion

The formation of renal stones consisting of pure struvite and or significant amounts of struvite is attributed to urinary infection by ureolytic bacteria. In addition, stones with a small proportion of struvite may suggest an E. coli infection, because this species has weak urease activity. However, there may be doubts about the infectious origin of stones when struvite is not present in a hydroxyapatite/calcium phosphocarbonate calculus. Recent studies showed that bacterial imprints were clearly present in some carbonated apatite stones, confirming that these stones developed in the presence of a bacterial infection, although not all apatite phosphate stones have an infectious origin. In fact, all of the 5 patients presented here who had stones with bacterial imprints had urinary infections by E. coli or K. pneumoniae. The reason for the absence of bacterial imprints in some struvite stones that are generated by ureolytic bacteria is uncertain, but may be attributed to the large size of struvite crystals. In particular, bacteria can be trapped by a deposit of small apatite crystals or spherulites but may not be retained by large struvite crystals, making it difficult to identify imprints in struvite stones. Notably, the formation of carbonated apatite calculi requires a urine pH greater than 6, at which apatite phosphates are insoluble. It is also important to consider that urine always contains nitrate, and that ureolytic bacteria reduce nitrate to nitrite. In fact, an acidic urine is required for the conversion of nitrite to nitric oxide (NO) and other nitrogen compounds that are toxic to a variety of microorganisms. Renal bacteria generally require a urinary pH above 6. A urinary pH greater than 6 reduces the solubility of apatite phosphates and favors bacterial development. For this reason, urinary stone formation in patients with distal renal tubular acidosis, a disorder that causes a persistent urinary pH higher than 6, is relatively common.

Struvite in kidney stones is an important marker for infection. Samples of struvite kidney stones were broken to obtain 38 pieces averaging 67 mg in weight, and these were randomized into four storage conditions: Airtight containers stored in the dark, open containers in the dark, open containers in ambient light, and open containers at elevated temperature (40°C).
