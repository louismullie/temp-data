## Mechanism of water loss on Mars

Mars has undergone significant water loss over its geological history, with multiple mechanisms contributing to this process. The **primary mechanisms** include:

- **Photolysis and thermal escape**: Ultraviolet (UV) radiation breaks water vapor into hydrogen and oxygen; hydrogen, being light, escapes to space via thermal escape, while oxygen remains or reacts with other species [^3df4a6c2] [^c87fee3c].
- **Solar wind stripping**: The solar wind directly interacts with the Martian atmosphere, stripping away atmospheric ions, including water-related ions, into space [^673b0df4].
- **Volcanic outgassing and thermal escape**: Volcanic activity releases water vapor, some of which can escape to space, though this is a secondary mechanism compared to photolysis and solar wind stripping [^2b9c4aa2].

---

## Evidence for photolysis and thermal escape

Photolysis and thermal escape are strongly supported by observational and theoretical evidence:

- **Hydrogen escape observations**: Spacecraft have directly observed hydrogen escaping from Mars's upper atmosphere, with escape rates varying seasonally and during dust storms, indicating ongoing water loss via photolysis [^c87fee3c] [^591682e9].
- **Isotopic evidence**: The deuterium-to-hydrogen (D/H) ratio in Martian water is significantly higher than Earth's, consistent with preferential loss of lighter hydrogen isotopes over geological time, supporting photolysis and escape as a dominant mechanism [^notfound].
- **Modeling studies**: Atmospheric models consistently show that photolysis and thermal escape can account for a large fraction of Mars's historical water loss, especially after the collapse of its global magnetic field, which exposed the atmosphere to increased solar radiation and solar wind [^3df4a6c2].

## Implications for Mars's climate and habitability

The loss of water via photolysis and thermal escape has profoundly affected Mars's climate and potential habitability. Water loss contributed to the **thinning of the atmosphere**, reducing the greenhouse effect and leading to the cold, dry conditions observed today [^3df4a6c2]. It also limited the stability of liquid water on the surface, constraining the duration and extent of potentially habitable environments [^b630926d].

### Anomalous transient enhancement of planetary ion escape at mars [^673b0df4]. Nature Communications (2025). High credibility.

Introduction

Mars has always been an important target in planetary science due to its divergent evolution from Earth. Ancient Mars is believed to have had a global dipole magnetic field, a thick atmosphere, and surface liquid water, similar to present-day Earth –. However, modern Mars lacks both a global dipole magnetic field and surface liquid water, possessing only a tenuous, CO 2 (carbon dioxide)-dominated atmosphere. This suggests that Mars has undergone significant atmospheric loss. An important factor contributing to this loss is the absence of a global dipole magnetic field, which allows the solar wind (a high-speed stream of charged particles emitted from the Sun) to directly strip away the upper Martian atmosphere and facilitate the escape of atmospheric ions into space –. Consequently, understanding how the solar wind energizes planetary ions and drives their escape is essential for comprehending the long-term atmospheric evolution of Mars, its climate change, and its potential habitability.

When the solar wind encounters Mars, the solar wind plasma and "frozen-in" interplanetary magnetic field (IMF) generate a motional electric field (E SW) in the Mars rest frame. This electric field initially accelerates the ionospheric ions, primarily O + (oxygen ions) and O 2 + (molecular oxygen ions), along its direction and subsequently deflects the ions anti-sunward. As a result, in the hemisphere where E SW points away from the planet, called +E hemisphere, the ions gradually gain energy as they ascend from the ionosphere. Thus, the ions have a high probability of becoming highly energized and escaping into space. These escaping and energized ions form a plume extending from the low-altitude ionosphere beyond the bow shock, contributing about 20%–30% to the total escape rate of ions –. Conversely, in the -E hemisphere where E SW points toward the planet, the dayside ionospheric ions are accelerated back toward the planet, causing many of these ions to return to the atmosphere instead of escaping to space.
