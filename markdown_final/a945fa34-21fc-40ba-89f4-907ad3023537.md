## Recommended dosing regimen

- **Initial dose**: 25/100 mg (carbidopa/levodopa) taken 1–2 hours before symptom onset or bedtime.

- **Maximum dose**: 200 mg levodopa per night (e.g. two 25/100 mg tablets or one 50/200 mg CR tablet).

- **Frequency**: Use intermittently, not daily, to minimize augmentation risk.

- **Titration**: Adjust to the lowest effective dose based on response and side effects [^115Ti7Jm].

## Clinical considerations and precautions

- **Augmentation risk**: High with chronic use [^114ZQk7J]; symptoms may start earlier, intensify, or spread.

- **Rebound**: Nighttime symptom return as levodopa wears off [^1165yTvj].

- **Long-term use**: Avoid chronic daily use [^116UF6xk]; consider alternatives if symptoms are frequent or severe [^112yEdtM].

- **Monitoring**: Regularly assess for augmentation and rebound; adjust or discontinue if they occur [^111bHbh3].

### Restless legs syndrome: Treatment with dopaminergic agents [^1165yTvj]. Neurology (2002). Low credibility.

Restless legs syndrome (RLS) is a common neurologic disorder affecting 5 to 10% of the population, with prevalence increasing as individuals age. The clinical hallmarks of RLS include dysesthesias or paresthesias in the legs and sometimes the arms, which occur primarily at rest. These symptoms are typically worse in the evening and are alleviated by movement. RLS can be a disabling disorder, causing sleep disturbances at night and excessive sleepiness during the day.

Although treatment with levodopa alleviates symptoms, many RLS patients develop rebound (the occurrence of symptoms during the night) or augmentation (the occurrence of symptoms before levodopa dosing in the evening). Augmentation occurs in up to 82% of patients treated with levodopa, limiting the long-term usefulness of this agent.

- **Direct dopamine receptor agonists**: These long-acting drugs are often administered as a single dose at bedtime. Among them, pergolide, pramipexole, ropinirole, and cabergoline have been shown to alleviate RLS symptoms in 70 to 100% of patients. The most common adverse effect is nausea. While augmentation may be associated with chronic agonist use, it is usually mild and responsive to additional dosing. The direct dopamine receptor agonists have largely replaced levodopa as the most effective treatment for RLS.
