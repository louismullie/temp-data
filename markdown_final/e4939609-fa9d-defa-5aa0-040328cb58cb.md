In select shortage scenarios, **expired cyclosporine (Neoral) may be used only if** a clinician determines there is no suitable alternative and the patient faces serious harm without therapy. This is supported by FDA's Shelf-Life Extension Program [^1d6d0566] and WHO guidance allowing conditional use of expired drugs when quality is verified and benefits outweigh risks. Cyclosporine is **high-risk** due to its narrow therapeutic index and potential for under- or over-immunosuppression; therefore, use requires strict storage verification, visual inspection, and close therapeutic drug monitoring. If expired cyclosporine is used, document the rationale, inform the patient, and monitor for efficacy and toxicity.

## Regulatory and professional guidance

- **FDA Shelf-Life Extension Program (SLEP)**: The FDA has historically extended expiration dates for certain medications in national stockpiles when stability data demonstrate continued potency and safety beyond the labeled expiry [^1d6d0566]. However, this program primarily applies to government stockpiles and does not automatically extend to outpatient or routine clinical use.

- **WHO guidance**: The WHO acknowledges that in exceptional circumstances, such as drug shortages, expired medications may be used if rigorous quality assessments are conducted and the benefits outweigh the risks. This includes visual inspection, stability data review, and clinical judgment.

- **Professional societies**: Major transplant and nephrology societies generally advise against routine use of expired immunosuppressants but recognize that in life-threatening shortages, individualized decisions may be necessary with enhanced monitoring.

---

## Risks associated with expired cyclosporine

- **Loss of potency**: Cyclosporine degradation can lead to subtherapeutic levels, increasing the risk of organ rejection or disease recurrence.

- **Toxicity from degradation products**: Although rare, degradation products may increase toxicity, particularly nephrotoxicity and neurotoxicity.

- **Formulation-specific risks**: Neoral's modified microemulsion formulation is sensitive to storage conditions; improper storage can accelerate degradation and alter pharmacokinetics.

---

## Evidence on stability of expired cyclosporine

There is **limited published data** on the stability of expired cyclosporine, particularly Neoral. Available data from the SLEP and other programs suggest that some medications retain potency beyond expiration, but this cannot be generalized to cyclosporine without specific testing. Cyclosporine's stability is highly dependent on storage conditions (temperature, humidity, light exposure), and degradation is accelerated by improper storage.

---

## Clinical scenarios where expired cyclosporine may be considered

Expired cyclosporine may be considered only in **exceptional circumstances** where:

- **Life-threatening situations**: The patient faces imminent risk of organ rejection, severe disease flare, or death without cyclosporine.

- **No suitable alternatives**: Alternative immunosuppressants (e.g. tacrolimus) are unavailable or contraindicated.

- **Verified storage conditions**: The expired medication has been stored under recommended conditions (room temperature, dry, away from light) and shows no signs of degradation (discoloration, odor, precipitation).

---

## Precautions and monitoring strategies

If expired cyclosporine is used, the following precautions are **essential**:

- **Visual inspection**: Check for discoloration, odor, precipitation, or other signs of degradation.

- **Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM)**: Measure cyclosporine trough levels frequently (every 2–3 days initially) to ensure therapeutic exposure.

- **Clinical monitoring**: Closely monitor for signs of rejection, nephrotoxicity, neurotoxicity, and infection.

- **Patient counseling**: Inform the patient of the risks and the rationale for using expired medication.

- **Documentation**: Record the rationale, storage conditions, and monitoring plan in the medical record.

---

## Alternative strategies during shortages

To mitigate the need for expired cyclosporine, consider the following **alternatives**:

- **Alternative immunosuppressants**: Tacrolimus, mycophenolate mofetil, or sirolimus may be substituted based on clinical context and availability.

- **Temporary dose adjustments**: Consult with transplant specialists or disease-specific experts to adjust immunosuppressive regimens temporarily.

- **Emergency supply chains**: Coordinate with pharmacies, manufacturers, and healthcare systems to secure non-expired supplies.

---

## Conclusion and recommendations

Expired cyclosporine (Neoral) should **generally be avoided** due to risks of reduced potency and potential toxicity. In exceptional shortage scenarios, it may be used only when:

- **No suitable alternatives exist**.
- **Storage conditions are verified**.
- **Rigorous monitoring is feasible**.

Clinicians should document the rationale, counsel patients, and monitor closely. If possible, prioritize securing non-expired alternatives through emergency supply chains or therapeutic substitutions.

### Cyclosporine (Neoral) [^36a3d17f]. FDA (2025). Medium credibility.

Conversion from Sandimmune to Neoral in Transplant Patients

In transplanted patients who are considered for conversion to Neoral from Sandimmune, Neoral should be started with the same daily dose as was previously used with Sandimmune (1:1 dose conversion). The Neoral dose should subsequently be adjusted to attain the pre-conversion cyclosporine blood trough concentration. Using the same trough concentration target range for Neoral as for Sandimmune results in greater cyclosporine exposure when Neoral is administered (see Pharmacokinetics, Absorption) . Patients with suspected poor absorption of Sandimmune require different dosing strategies (see Transplant Patients with Poor Absorption of Sandimmune, below) . In some patients, the increase in blood trough concentration is more pronounced and may be of clinical significance.

Until the blood trough concentration attains the pre-conversion value, it is strongly recommended that the cyclosporine blood trough concentration be monitored every 4 to 7 days after conversion to Neoral. In addition, clinical safety parameters, such as serum creatinine and blood pressure, should be monitored every two weeks during the first two months after conversion. If the blood trough concentrations are outside the desired range and/or if the clinical safety parameters worsen, the dosage of Neoral must be adjusted accordingly.

Transplant Patients with Poor Absorption of Sandimmune

Patients with lower than expected cyclosporine blood trough concentrations in relation to the oral dose of Sandimmune may have poor or inconsistent absorption of cyclosporine from Sandimmune. After conversion to Neoral, patients tend to have higher cyclosporine concentrations. Due to the increase in bioavailability of cyclosporine following conversion to Neoral, the cyclosporine blood trough concentration may exceed the target range. Particular caution should be exercised when converting patients to Neoral at doses greater than 10 mg/kg/day. The dose of Neoral should be titrated individually based on cyclosporine trough concentrations, tolerability, and clinical response. In this population the cyclosporine blood trough concentration should be measured more frequently, at least twice a week (daily, if initial dose exceeds 10 mg/kg/day) until the concentration stabilizes within the desired range.

### Cyclosporine (Neoral) [^96147d76]. FDA (2025). Medium credibility.

CLINICAL PHARMACOLOGY

Cyclosporine is a potent immunosuppressive agent that in animals prolongs survival of allogeneic transplants involving skin, kidney, liver, heart, pancreas, bone marrow, small intestine, and lung. Cyclosporine has been demonstrated to suppress some humoral immunity and to a greater extent, cell-mediated immune reactions such as allograft rejection, delayed hypersensitivity, experimental allergic encephalomyelitis, Freund’s adjuvant arthritis, and graft versus host disease in many animal species for a variety of organs.

The effectiveness of cyclosporine results from specific and reversible inhibition of immunocompetent lymphocytes in the G0- and G1-phase of the cell cycle. T-lymphocytes are preferentially inhibited. The T-helper cell is the main target, although the T-suppressor cell may also be suppressed. Cyclosporine also inhibits lymphokine production and release, including interleukin-2.

No effects on phagocytic function (changes in enzyme secretions, chemotactic migration of granulocytes, macrophage migration, carbon clearance in vivo) have been detected in animals. Cyclosporine does not cause bone marrow suppression in animal models or man.

### Cyclosporine (Neoral) [^288e36a1]. FDA (2025). Medium credibility.

There is an increased risk for the development of skin and lymphoproliferative malignancies in cyclosporine-treated psoriasis patients. The relative risk of malignancies is comparable to that observed in psoriasis patients treated with other immunosuppressive agents.

Tumors were reported in 32 (2.2%) of 1439 psoriasis patients treated with cyclosporine worldwide from clinical trials. Additional tumors have been reported in 7 patients in cyclosporine postmarketing experience. Skin malignancies were reported in 16 (1.1%) of these patients; all but 2 of them had previously received PUVA therapy. Methotrexate was received by 7 patients. UVB and coal tar had been used by 2 and 3 patients, respectively. Seven patients had either a history of previous skin cancer or a potentially predisposing lesion was present prior to cyclosporine exposure. Of the 16 patients with skin cancer, 11 patients had 18 squamous cell carcinomas and 7 patients had 10 basal cell carcinomas.

There were two lymphoproliferative malignancies; one case of non-Hodgkin’s lymphoma which required chemotherapy, and one case of mycosis fungoides which regressed spontaneously upon discontinuation of cyclosporine. There were four cases of benign lymphocytic infiltration: 3 regressed spontaneously upon discontinuation of cyclosporine, while the fourth regressed despite continuation of the drug. The remainder of the malignancies, 13 cases (0.9%), involved various organs.

Patients should not be treated concurrently with cyclosporine and PUVA or UVB, other radiation therapy, or other immunosuppressive agents, because of the possibility of excessive immunosuppression and the subsequent risk of malignancies (see CONTRAINDICATIONS) . Patients should also be warned to protect themselves appropriately when in the sun, and to avoid excessive sun exposure. Patients should be thoroughly evaluated before and during treatment for the presence of malignancies remembering that malignant lesions may be hidden by psoriatic plaques. Skin lesions not typical of psoriasis should be biopsied before starting treatment. Patients should be treated with Neoral only after complete resolution of suspicious lesions, and only if there are no other treatment options (see Special Monitoring for Psoriasis Patients) .

Strengths and limitations

Our review’s strengths include the use of duplicate screening and data extraction, the use of the most updated GRADE guidance, assessed by methodologists with significant experience in assessing interventional studies. This is critical for any meta-analysis, as poor application of methods from the statistical analysis to quality assessment can obfuscate the interpretation of the evidence for clinicians and patient partners. Furthermore, our review was done in pairs and in duplicate, reducing the risk of errors when screening and collecting data. Lastly, there was very little heterogeneity across the trials included, with some exception regarding the definition of CLAD vs BOS.

There are also notable limitations. First, all trials included in the analysis did not address the question of multi-organ transplantation. Although the literature suggests tacrolimus is more effective than cyclosporine in other solid organ transplantation as well, we cannot assume this would translate to similar benefit in the multi-organ setting.

Second, although we judged the lack of blinding to be unlikely to affect the outcome of the trials, it is possible that the lack of blinding has inflated the overall benefit of tacrolimus compared with cyclosporine due to unmeasured or unconsidered confounders.

Third, we pooled the results of trials that reported on both BOS and CLAD. It should be noted that the definition of CLAD encompasses BOS. Since 2012, these terms have essentially become equivalent. Because of this, we chose not to downgrade the evidence for indirectness. This is because BOS accounts for approximately 60–75% of all CLAD cases, making it a significant component when comparing the effects of tacrolimus and cyclosporine on CLAD.

Fourth, included trials used mycophenolate-based background immunosuppression, and there was variability in the use of induction agent (see Table S3). It is unknown whether different background therapy would result in differences in efficacy. Furthermore, the follow-up duration of the included trials, which ranged from 1.39 to 3 years. This timeframe may be insufficient to fully capture long-term adverse effects associated with immunosuppression, particularly the development of CLAD. Given that CLAD often manifests beyond this follow-up window, the observed differences between tacrolimus and cyclosporine might not reflect the true long-term outcomes. Lastly, subgroup analysis was limited by the reported data, and important subgroup effects based on aetiology of transplant and double vs single lung transplant are uncertain.
