## Clinical significance of abnormal urinalysis with negative culture

- **Leukocyte esterase and proteinuria**: These can reflect transient inflammation, fever, dehydration, or contamination, especially in young children [^1126CRh8].

- **Trace hematuria**: Commonly transient and benign in children; often from minor trauma, fever, or contamination [^1126CRh8].

- **Negative culture**: Strongly argues against UTI, especially with a properly collected catheterized specimen [^117W6oQy] [^115WLzwX].

## Clinical guidelines and recommendations

AAP guidelines recommend obtaining a urinalysis and culture in well-appearing febrile infants 8–60 days old and **treating only when the culture is positive**. ACEP guidance for febrile infants 2 months to 2 years similarly advises obtaining a urinalysis and culture when UTI is suspected and **treating only if the culture is positive**. IDSA guidelines recommend **against treating asymptomatic bacteriuria** or an abnormal urinalysis in the absence of a positive culture [^114DUJ5V].

## Potential risks of unnecessary repeat urinalysis

- **Antibiotic overuse**: Unnecessary antibiotics increase resistance and adverse effects [^1126oELy].

## When to consider repeat urinalysis

Repeat urinalysis is indicated only if:

- **Persistent symptoms**: Fever, emesis, or new urinary symptoms persist or recur.

- **Clinical deterioration**: The child's condition worsens or new symptoms develop.

- **High-risk factors**: Known urinary tract anomalies or immunocompromise.

### Urine dipstick for the diagnosis of urinary tract infection in febrile infants aged 2 to 6 months [^115WLzwX]. Pediatrics (2025). High credibility.
