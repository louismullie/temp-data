## Antiplatelet therapy

### Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT)

- **Regimen**: Aspirin 75–325 mg daily plus clopidogrel 75 mg daily [^114L4YH9] [^1129JDJC].
- **Duration**: Continue for at least 4 weeks post-procedure [^114L4YH9] [^1129JDJC].
- **Rationale**: DAPT reduces periprocedural stroke, stent thrombosis, and restenosis [^111V6LgG] [^116MHkQA].

---

### Transition to single antiplatelet therapy

After 4 weeks, continue aspirin or clopidogrel indefinitely [^114L4YH9] [^113UURwz]; **clopidogrel 75 mg daily** is often preferred long term.

---

### Alternative antiplatelet agents

Consider **ticagrelor** for clopidogrel resistance or intolerance [^11571APU] [^114jhKxB]. **GPI inhibitors** are rarely used and reserved for high thrombotic risk or clopidogrel resistance [^117PpKha] [^112ZsWT9].

---

## Statin therapy

Initiate or continue **high-intensity statin therapy** (e.g. atorvastatin 40–80 mg or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg daily). Statins lower LDL, stabilize plaque, and reduce restenosis and ischemic events [^114L4YH9] [^114UMydS].

---

## Blood pressure management

Maintain a **systolic BP < 140 mmHg** to prevent hyperperfusion syndrome and hemorrhagic complications; use beta-blockers, ACE inhibitors, or calcium channel blockers as appropriate.

## Monitoring and follow-up

### Neurological monitoring

Perform frequent **neurologic checks** during the first 24–48 hours to detect stroke or TIA [^112xs31f].

---

### Imaging surveillance

Obtain **duplex ultrasound** at 1, 6, and 12 months, then annually, to assess stent patency and detect restenosis [^113T8GM1].

---

### Laboratory monitoring

Consider **platelet function testing** in high-risk patients or when antiplatelet resistance is suspected [^111LYvhU] [^11571APU].

## Management of complications

### Bleeding complications

Management includes adjusting **antiplatelet therapy**, using **proton pump inhibitors**, and monitoring hemoglobin/hematocrit [^111HvjdF] [^116MHkQA].

---

### Thrombotic complications

Management involves intensifying **antiplatelet therapy** or considering **anticoagulation** in select cases.

---

### Hyperperfusion syndrome

Management entails **strict BP control** and close neurologic monitoring.

### Comparative effectiveness of carotid stenting to medical therapy among patients with asymptomatic carotid stenosis [^114UT6YY]. Stroke (2022). Low credibility.

Carotid artery stenosis accounts for approximately 11% of strokes worldwide. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) conducted over two decades have established that carotid endarterectomy (CEA) is beneficial in preventing stroke in both asymptomatic and symptomatic patients with carotid stenosis. However, no completed trial is available that directly compares carotid artery stenting (CAS) to medical therapy (MT). One Europe-based trial was halted due to inadequate recruitment.

Carotid revascularization among asymptomatic patients is primarily a prevention procedure. Revascularization represents a trade-off between higher perioperative short-term risks, including stroke and death, in exchange for a lower long-term risk of stroke. The risk of stroke from carotid artery stenosis has been declining since the original asymptomatic carotid trials were completed, due to marked improvements in MT. Stroke has now dropped from the third to the fifth leading cause of death in the United States.

Given the declining stroke risk from asymptomatic carotid artery stenosis, the National Institute of Neurological Disorders and Stroke funded the CREST-2 (Carotid Revascularization and Medical Management for Asymptomatic Carotid Stenosis Trial) to determine whether revascularization offers any benefit beyond aggressive risk factor control. CREST-2 was launched in 2014 and is an ongoing trial involving two parallel RCTs: (1) CEA plus intensive MT versus intensive MT alone, and (2) CAS plus intensive MT versus intensive MT alone. CREST-2 has a projected completion date in 2025.
