## Avantages cliniques de la chirurgie robotique en thoracique

### Amélioration de la visualisation et de la précision chirurgicale

La vision 3D haute définition et le grossissement améliorent la visualisation anatomique [^115p7PHC], permettant une dissection plus précise et une réduction des complications intraopératoires [^117U4xSE]. Les instruments articulés à 7 degrés de liberté offrent une **dexterité supérieure** à celle de la VATS, facilitant des manoeuvres complexes dans des espaces confinés [^1168iJbM].

---

### Réduction de la perte sanguine et des complications postopératoires

Les études cliniques montrent une **perte sanguine significativement réduite** [^1135oQ8A] par rapport à la VATS et à la chirurgie ouverte [^113XFeoi]. Les taux de complications postopératoires, notamment les infections et les complications respiratoires, sont également réduits [^116saGr3].

---

### Réduction de la douleur postopératoire et de la durée d'hospitalisation

L'approche minimalement invasive de la chirurgie robotique entraîne une **réduction de la douleur postopératoire** [^notfound], permettant une récupération plus rapide et une mobilité accrue des patients [^11244grq]. La durée d'hospitalisation est significativement réduite [^116saGr3], ce qui peut contribuer à une meilleure utilisation des ressources hospitalières [^notfound].

---

### Amélioration de l'ergonomie chirurgicale

Le poste de commande ergonomique réduit la fatigue du chirurgien et améliore le confort pendant les longues interventions [^116f5YyM], ce qui peut **réduire le risque d'erreurs chirurgicales** liées à la fatigue [^113XFeoi].

## Applications spécifiques en chirurgie thoracique

### Lobectomie et segmentectomie pulmonaires

La chirurgie robotique est largement utilisée pour la **lobectomie et la segmentectomie pulmonaires** [^116jrasR], notamment dans le traitement du cancer du poumon non à petites cellules (NSCLC) [^116hrQjq]. Les études cliniques montrent des résultats oncologiques comparables à ceux de la VATS et de la chirurgie ouverte [^115dCS4y], avec une réduction de la perte sanguine et une durée d'hospitalisation plus courte [^113XFeoi].

---

### Oesophagectomie

La chirurgie robotique est utilisée pour l'oesophagectomie, permettant une dissection précise des structures anatomiques complexes [^114hmVBw]. Les études cliniques montrent une **réduction de la durée de l'intervention** [^1135oQ8A] et une amélioration des résultats postopératoires par rapport à la VATS [^113XFeoi].

---

### Chirurgie médiastinale

La chirurgie robotique est utilisée pour la résection de masses médiastinales, offrant une **meilleure visualisation et une précision accrue** [^11244grq], ce qui réduit le risque de complications intraopératoires [^117U4xSE].

## Limites et défis de la chirurgie robotique en thoracique

### Coûts élevés

L'acquisition et la maintenance des systèmes robotiques entraînent des **coûts significativement plus élevés** [^111ZCyM9] que la VATS et la chirurgie ouverte [^116hrQjq]. Ces coûts élevés peuvent limiter l'accessibilité de la chirurgie robotique, particulièrement dans les établissements de santé à ressources limitées [^116EUNxV].

---

### Courbe d'apprentissage

La chirurgie robotique nécessite une formation spécifique et une période d'apprentissage [^111EU45V], ce qui peut **augmenter initialement la durée des interventions** et le risque de complications [^112rhdYZ]. Des programmes de formation standardisés sont essentiels pour surmonter cette limitation [^111cWuxZ].

---

### Absence de preuve de supériorité absolue

Bien que la chirurgie robotique offre plusieurs avantages cliniques, **aucune étude n'a démontré une supériorité absolue** par rapport à la VATS ou à la chirurgie ouverte en termes de résultats oncologiques à long terme [^111s6jVp]. Des études supplémentaires sont nécessaires pour établir clairement la supériorité de la chirurgie robotique [^116f5YyM].

### Robot-assisted surgery in thoracic and visceral indications: an updated systematic review [^11244grq]. Surgical Endoscopy (2024). Medium credibility.

Robot-assisted surgery (RAS) is an advanced minimally invasive procedure that is nowadays utilised in a wide clinical spectrum. The robotic system's instruments are manipulated through a direct telemanipulator, which acts as a remote device enabling the surgeon to execute typical surgical motions by controlling the robotic arms. Compared to conventional minimally invasive techniques, RAS is expected to offer surgical benefits in terms of visualization, dexterity, and ergonomics, while still retaining the perioperative advantages of endoscopic surgery. Moreover, RAS might be associated with a variety of potential advantages for patients such as a shortened hospitalisation, fastened recovery times, reduced risk of complications as well as less traumatic access into the body.

Despite anticipated benefits, RAS has not proven its superiority over conventional techniques in numerous surgical procedures. Additionally, the annual procedure volume of RAS has significantly increased from 136,000 in 2008 to 877,000 in 2017. Hence, the European Network for Health Technology Assessment (EUnetHTA) conducted a systematic report in 2019 to evaluate the effectiveness and safety of RAS in 14 thoracic and visceral indications. Notwithstanding the increasing use of RAS, a scarcity of reliable evidence was noted across almost all assessed indications, with minor improvements in clinical outcomes observed in only four procedures.

Currently, new technological advancements, including endoluminal robotics and the integration of artificial intelligence into robotic systems, are underway, illustrating the dynamic landscape of surgical progress. This trend poses additional challenges in ensuring that advancements align with evidence-based practices, requiring a careful balance between embracing new technologies and ensuring their efficacy and safety through rigorous research and comparative studies. The combination of these factors, along with the numerous ongoing studies identified in the 2019 report, underscores the necessity for a reassessment of the clinical effectiveness and safety of RAS. Therefore, our systematic review aims to provide an update on the evidence identified in the EUnetHTA report, focusing on 14 thoracic and visceral procedures in comparison to laparoscopic and open surgery.

### Robot-assisted surgery in thoracic and visceral indications: an updated systematic review [^113XFeoi]. Surgical Endoscopy (2024). Medium credibility.

Discussion

The rapid introduction of expensive technological advances poses a challenge for policymakers as it often surpasses the capacity of governments and society to promptly adapt to the resulting changes, leading to heightened inequalities and ethical dilemmas. Thus, the integration of new technologies frequently precedes the accumulation of solid evidence demonstrating clear superiority. This phenomenon is exemplified by the fast diffusion of robot-assisted prostatectomy in Europe, demonstrating a substantial increase in performed procedures from 3% in 2008 (Germany) to 46% in 2018, highlighting the early adoption of advanced surgical techniques without an initial robust evidence base.

Nowadays RAS is introduced into a variety of surgical specialities with immense evidence generated solely on Intuitive-Surgical products of 13,500 peer-reviewed articles, averaging one publication every 4 h. Despite this huge evidence base and the broad application, it is still unknown if RAS overcomes the limitations of conventional approaches. Therefore, we aimed to systematically analyse the effectiveness and safety of RAS in 14 thoracic and visceral indications compared to laparoscopic or open surgery. For nine indications of interest, a total of 20 RCTs and five follow-up publications could be identified. In general, the overall quality of evidence was low with more than half of the studies (n = 12) exhibiting a high risk of bias.

In summary, among the investigated study endpoints, rectal and liver resections were the only indications that demonstrated advantages with RAS over conventional procedures in at least four outcomes of interest. Notwithstanding claims of superiority, statistically significant differences favouring RAS were solely observed in one respective indication across several investigated outcomes — recurrence, QoL, transfusions, and hospitalization (Table 2). Notably, safety outcomes were enhanced for patients undergoing robot-assisted gastrectomy, as well as rectal and liver resection. Moreover, operation time yielded conflicting results; shortened surgery times were observed exclusively in robot-assisted liver resections and fundoplications, while five indications showed prolonged surgeries. Additionally, reduced blood loss was observed in the intervention groups of five indications: lung lobectomy, oesophagectomy, rectal resection, liver resection, and gastrectomy. Overall, we could see that in a great deal of instances, patient-relevant outcomes were not available or comparable to the results of the control standard intervention.

### Consensus statement on definitions and nomenclature for robotic thoracic surgery [^116rRWJ6]. The Journal of Thoracic and Cardiovascular Surgery (2017). Low credibility.

Objectives

Robotic thoracic operations are increasing, and new robotic systems are imminent. A definition of what constitutes a robotic thoracic operation and a nomenclature to detail the technique used is needed to accurately compare outcomes.

Methods

The American Association of Thoracic Surgeons Guideline Committee appointed an expert consensus writing committee to construct definitions and nomenclature for robotic thoracic surgery. A PubMed search was generated and after vetting and review of the literature a consensus statement was reached.

Results

The proposed definition is: "A robotic thoracic operation is a minimally invasive surgical procedure that does not spread, lift or remove any part of the chest or abdominal wall and is characterized by: the surgeon and the assistant's vision of the operative field is via a monitor only and the patient's tissue is manipulated by robotic instruments that follow a slave like mimic of human hands or thoughts via a computerized system". In addition, a flexible nomenclature is proposed that should be applicable to current and future robotic systems that details the number of robotic arms used, the types of ports and/or incisions made, the use of insufflation, and the operation performed.

Conclusions

The American Association of Thoracic Surgeons writing committee proposes a definition and nomenclature for robotic thoracic surgery. Definitions are needed to ensure that future studies accurately compare results and outcomes and nomenclatures allow surgeons and scientists from diverse countries and cultures to use the same language to allow accurate communication.

Assessment and feedback for robotic thoracic training — To prepare for cases, trainees should familiarize themselves with standard anatomy, anatomic variations, and educational videos; additionally, they should review operative steps, focus on exposure and executing technical maneuvers, deconstruct steps before surgery, and analyze the postoperative video for feedback. Methods

Background

There may be equivalent efficacy of the lymph node evaluation for minimally invasive lobectomy compared with open lobectomy for stage I non-small cell lung cancer. Methods

This retrospective study analyzed 24257 patients with clinical stage T2–3N0M0 non-small cell lung cancer from the National Cancer Database.
