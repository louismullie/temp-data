## MRI findings in normal pressure hydrocephalus

MRI is the **gold standard** for diagnosing NPH, with characteristic findings that distinguish it from other neurodegenerative or structural brain disorders [^notfound]. The following MRI features are typically observed in NPH:

- **Dilated ventricles**: Enlargement of the lateral and third ventricles, often disproportionate to cortical atrophy, is a hallmark of NPH [^113qsdET] [^111FUPAi].

- **Periventricular hyperintensities**: On T2-weighted or FLAIR sequences, periventricular white matter hyperintensities indicate transependymal CSF flow, reflecting chronic CSF accumulation [^112eiibj].

- **Callosal angle**: A callosal angle less than 90 degrees on coronal MRI is highly suggestive of NPH [^1137w1tU].

- **Aqueductal flow void**: Prominent CSF flow void in the aqueduct of Sylvius on T2-weighted images indicates increased CSF velocity, supporting the diagnosis of NPH [^112eiibj].

- **Disproportionately enlarged subarachnoid space hydrocephalus (DESH)**: Narrowing of the subarachnoid space over the convexities with enlarged Sylvian fissures and basal cisterns is a characteristic pattern of NPH [^1137w1tU].

These MRI findings, particularly ventriculomegaly with minimal cortical atrophy and the presence of DESH, strongly support the diagnosis of NPH in this patient [^111FUPAi].

## Treatment of normal pressure hydrocephalus

The primary treatment for NPH is **surgical CSF diversion**, typically via a ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt [^115psGeX]. Shunting reduces ventricular size, relieves pressure on brain structures, and improves neurological function. Gait disturbance is the symptom most likely to improve after shunting, with reported **improvement rates ranging from 60% to 80%** [^115in3xh] [^1115rjmC]. Cognitive impairment also improves in **approximately 50% of patients**, particularly when treated early in the disease course [^11279X4P] [^115in3xh]. Urinary incontinence is less responsive to shunting, with improvement observed in **about 30% of patients** [^115in3xh].

---

## Alternative treatments and considerations

While shunting is the **standard treatment**, alternative or adjunctive therapies may be considered in specific cases:

- **Endoscopic third ventriculostomy (ETV)**: ETV is an alternative surgical option, particularly in patients with aqueductal stenosis or obstructive hydrocephalus. However, its efficacy in NPH is less well-established than VP shunting [^notfound].

- **Lumbar puncture (tap test)**: A large-volume lumbar puncture can temporarily relieve symptoms and predict shunt responsiveness. A positive response (improvement in gait or cognition) supports the diagnosis and likelihood of benefit from shunting [^115psGeX].

- **Medical management**: Conservative management with medications (e.g. acetazolamide, furosemide) may temporarily reduce CSF production but is generally ineffective in providing sustained symptom relief in NPH [^115vmUoR].

---

## Prognosis and outcomes

The prognosis of **NPH** varies depending on the severity of symptoms, duration of disease, and patient comorbidities. Early diagnosis and intervention significantly improve outcomes, particularly for gait and cognitive symptoms [^115in3xh]. Long-term studies indicate that **approximately 50% of patients** experience sustained improvement after shunting, while the remaining patients may have partial or no improvement. Complications of shunting include infection, shunt malfunction, and subdural hematoma, occurring in **approximately 10–20% of cases** [^113BYs5V] [^112nY7Z9].

Gait instability, urinary incontinence, and dementia are the signs and symptoms typically found in patients who have normal pressure hydrocephalus. Estimated to cause no more than 5 percent of cases of dementia, normal pressure hydrocephalus often is treatable, and accurate recognition of the clinical triad coupled with radiographic evidence most commonly identifies likely responders. Magnetic resonance imaging or computed tomography typically demonstrates ventricular dilation with preservation of the surrounding brain tissue. The abnormality in normal pressure hydrocephalus occurs secondary to an abnormality in fluid removal, leading to an increase in ventricular size and encroachment of enlarged ventricles on adjacent brain tissue. The pressure exerted on the cerebral parenchyma by immense fluid-filled cavities deforms white matter tracts, instigating gait abnormalities and incomplete control of the bladder, as well as difficulties in processing incoming stimulation and in producing expeditious responses. Signs and symptoms often occur as sequelae to an imbalance between the expected ongoing production of cerebrospinal fluid and continuous efflux. Ventriculoperitoneal shunting is used to relieve excess ventricular fluid not absorbed by normal physiologic channels. It is currently divided into primary NPH, with no identifiable cause, and secondary NPH, linked to previous intracranial insults.

The diagnosis of primary NPH is guided by two major criteria sets. The Japanese guideline categorizes NPH as possible, probable, and definite, whereas the International guideline classifies it as unlikely, possible, and probable". Unlikely NPH" refers to cases where symptoms are better explained by other causes. For "possible NPH", the Japanese guideline requires at least two symptoms from the triad, excluding atypical or secondary features, whereas the International guideline requires at least one symptom, which may be accompanied by atypical features. For "probable NPH", both guidelines require at least two symptoms (particularly gait disturbance), normal cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) opening pressure, and specific neuroimaging features (or symptom improvement after a CSF drainage test in the Japanese guideline). "Definite NPH" refers to individuals who improve after shunt surgery.

The prevalence of NPH increases with age, ranging from 0.003% in individuals aged 50–59 yearsto 5.9% in those aged 80 years and older. Martín‐Láez et alsuggested that 60 years could be considered the threshold for a population "at risk for NPH", given its prevalence is 15‐fold higher in the seventh decade of life compared with the sixth. Although familial NPH has been underexplored, 4.8% of patients with NPH reported a relative who underwent shunt placementand approximately 7% of first‐degree relatives exhibit two or more NPH triad symptoms. Similarly, the genetic factors contributing to NPH risk or protection remain poorly understood and have not been systematically investigated.

In this context, the aim of this systematic review is to assess the contribution of genetics to NPH risk by analyzing the existing literature on familial cases, genetic variants linked to primary NPH, and co‐occurring neurogenetic disorders.
