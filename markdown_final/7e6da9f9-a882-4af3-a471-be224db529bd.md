- **Triggering episode**: Triggered by an episode of unsteadiness, dizziness, or vertigo, caused by another balance disorder, a neurological or medical disorder, or psychological distress.
- **Symptoms must cause considerable distress**: Significant distress to the sufferer is required.
- **Exclusion criteria**: The symptoms should not be better accounted for by an alternative diagnosis.

The following constitutes key background information on motion sickness:

- **Definition**: Motion sickness is a common condition that results from an adverse physiologic response to real or perceived motion stimuli, typically involving low-frequency vertical, lateral, angular, or rotary motion occurring during travel by sea, land, air, or immersion in virtual reality.

- **Pathophysiology**: The pathophysiology of motion sickness involves a conflict between expected and actual interactions among vestibular, visual, and proprioceptive inputs. - **Disease course**: The clinical course of motion sickness typically begins with a feeling of unease, followed by cold sweats and dizziness, progressing to nausea and, in severe cases.
