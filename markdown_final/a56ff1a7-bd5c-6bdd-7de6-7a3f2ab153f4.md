Checking the pulse before giving digoxin is a **standard safety practice** to detect bradycardia or conduction block, which are key precursors to digoxin toxicity [^40961365]. This step is supported by FDA labeling that advises monitoring for toxicity and clinical response, and by guidelines recommending digoxin immune Fab for bradycardia or hemodynamic compromise from digoxin toxicity [^93ced49f]. ## Pharmacological rationale for pulse monitoring

Digoxin increases vagal tone and slows AV nodal conduction, so **bradycardia and conduction block** are common toxic effects, especially in patients with pre-existing conduction disease or those on beta-blockers or calcium channel blockers [^40961365]. Bradycardia or new conduction abnormalities may signal impending toxicity and warrant withholding the dose and reassessing the patient [^cf605a83].

---

## Clinical evidence supporting pulse monitoring

Direct RCTs specifically testing pre-dose pulse checks are lacking; however, **indirect evidence and expert consensus** support the practice:

- **FDA labeling**: Recommends monitoring for signs of toxicity and clinical response, with serum digoxin levels interpreted in context; toxicity risk rises with levels > 2 ng/mL, and bradycardia is a key clinical sign [^3083c40f].
- **Guidelines**: ACC/AHA/HRS recommend digoxin immune Fab for bradycardia or hemodynamic compromise from digoxin toxicity, underscoring the clinical importance of detecting bradyarrhythmias early [^93ced49f].
- **Implementation data**: A multisite study showed that electronic medication systems improved pulse/BP checking before digoxin by 40.3%, correlating with fewer procedural errors and better adherence to safety practices [^428b06ab].

## Risks associated with omitting pulse monitoring

Failing to check the pulse can allow **undetected bradycardia or conduction block**, increasing the risk of:

- **Progression to high-grade AV block**: Potentially requiring pacing or reversal with Fab [^93ced49f].
- **Ventricular arrhythmias**: Including VT/VF, which can be fatal [^b5dfdf27].
- **Clinical deterioration**: Particularly in patients with renal impairment, electrolyte disturbances, or interacting drugs [^cf605a83].

## Practical clinical implications

Given digoxin's narrow therapeutic index and toxicity risk, **checking the pulse before each dose** is a simple, effective way to screen for bradycardia or conduction disease. If the pulse is < 60 bpm or irregular, withhold the dose, assess for toxicity, and consider an ECG or serum digoxin level before restarting [^notfound].

## Conclusion and recommendations

Checking the pulse before digoxin is a **standard safety practice** supported by pharmacology, regulatory guidance, and implementation data, even though direct RCTs are lacking. Clinicians should:

- **Check the pulse**: Before each dose, especially in patients with conduction disease, renal impairment, or interacting drugs.
- **Withhold if abnormal**: Hold for bradycardia, irregular rhythm, or new conduction abnormalities, then reassess.
- **Monitor closely**: Use ECG or serum levels when toxicity is suspected or dosing changes occur [^3083c40f].

This approach balances evidence, safety, and practicality, aligning with best practice for a high-risk medication.

### Consumer wearable devices for evaluation of heart rate control using digoxin versus β-blockers: the RATE-AF randomized trial [^9644e335]. Nature Medicine (2024). Excellent credibility.

Methods

The RATE-AF trial

The RATE-AF trial was a prospective, randomized, open-label, blinded end-point trial that compared the use of low-dose digoxin versus beta-blockers for long-term heart rate control. Recruitment took place across primary care sites and three hospitals in the West Midlands region of England between 2016 and 2018. Inclusion criteria were: (1) age 60 years or older; (2) permanent AF in need of rate control; and (3) symptoms of heart failure, with breathlessness equivalent to NYHA class II or above. Exclusion criteria were limited so that the trial population reflected routine clinical practice (see published protocol for full list of selection criteria).

Ethics and inclusion statement

The trial was co-designed by a patient and public involvement (PPI) team, with the aim of improving quality of life for patients with AF. Ethical approval was obtained from the East Midlands–Derby Research Ethics Committee (16/EM/0178), the Health Research Authority and the Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Agency. The trial was publicly funded by the UK National Institute for Health and Care Research and registered with clinicaltrials.gov and clinicaltrialsregister.eu.

Randomization and trial process

Each participant was randomized to either digoxin 62.5–250 µg or bisoprolol 1.25–10 mg once daily in a 1:1 ratio at their baseline visit. Randomization was completed using a computer-generated minimization algorithm to ensure treatment arms were balanced for gender and AF symptoms, based on the mEHRA classification.

The trial was embedded into usual care within the National Health Service (NHS), with participants attending formal visits at baseline, 6 months and 12 months. Endpoints acquired were patient-reported quality of life, NT-proBNP, symptoms and functional capacity using mEHRA and NYHA class, 6MW distance and time, heart rate (pulse examination), 12-lead ECG, LVEF using cardiac ultrasound and assessment of adverse events.
