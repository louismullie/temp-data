Nutritional needs in chronic SCI are shaped by **lower energy expenditure** [^61067be7] and **altered body composition**, with resting energy expenditure ~14–27% below able-bodied levels [^eec78ce1]. Total energy needs are typically **1,500–2,000 kcal/day** [^notfound], adjusted to activity and injury level; protein should be **1.2–1.5 g/kg/day** to counteract muscle loss [^notfound], and fiber should be **25–35 g/day** to support bowel function [^notfound]. Vitamin D and calcium are often deficient and require supplementation, and fluid intake should be **~30 mL/kg/day** unless restricted [^3be517db]. Nutrition should be individualized, with regular screening for malnutrition and obesity, and coordinated with bowel and pressure injury prevention strategies [^c846d241] [^8e44e1de].

## Energy requirements

- **Resting energy expenditure (REE)**: 14–27% lower than able-bodied individuals due to reduced muscle mass and metabolic rate [^eec78ce1] [^61067be7].
- **Total energy expenditure (TEE)**: typically 1,500–2,000 kcal/day, depending on activity, injury level, and body composition [^notfound].
- **Adjustment factors**: tetraplegia requires ~10% less energy than paraplegia; adjust intake to prevent obesity or undernutrition [^2edf40c2].

## Macronutrient requirements

| **Macronutrient** | **Recommended intake** | **Rationale** |
|-|-|-|
| Protein | 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day | Counteracts muscle atrophy; supports tissue repair and immune function [^notfound] |
| Carbohydrates | 45–65% of total calories | Primary energy source; emphasize complex carbs and fiber [^notfound] |
| Fat | 20–35% of total calories | Limit saturated fat; prioritize unsaturated fats for cardiovascular health [^notfound] |
| Fiber | 25–35 g/day | Supports bowel management and glycemic control [^notfound] |

## Micronutrient requirements

- **Vitamin D**: Frequently deficient due to limited sun exposure; supplement to maintain serum 25(OH)D ≥ 30 ng/mL [^423cfe1a] [^fb5d58a0].
- **Calcium**: 1,000–1,200 mg/day to support bone health and prevent osteoporosis [^notfound].
- **Vitamin B12**: Monitor and supplement if deficient, especially in older adults or those on proton pump inhibitors [^notfound].
- **Iron**: Monitor for deficiency or excess; supplementation based on lab results and clinical context [^00782b25].

---

## Fluid requirements

Fluid intake should be **~30 mL/kg/day** (about 2–3 liters for most adults), adjusted for comorbidities (e.g. heart/renal failure) and environmental factors [^3be517db].

---

## Special considerations

- **Neurogenic bowel**: Fiber, fluids, and timing are critical; coordinate with bowel programs and medications [^3a43ec65] [^4286cf00].
- **Pressure injuries**: Adequate protein, vitamin C, and zinc support healing; avoid overfeeding [^c846d241] [^8e44e1de].
- **Obesity**: Common due to reduced energy needs; emphasize energy deficit, portion control, and regular activity [^eec78ce1] [^e54abda2].

---

## Nutritional screening and assessment

Regular screening should use validated tools such as the **Spinal Nutrition Screening Tool (SNST)** or the **Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST)** to detect malnutrition and obesity risk [^d02cfbf2] [^bc8479f9]. Assessment should include dietary intake, weight/BMI, body composition, and lab markers (e.g. albumin, prealbumin, vitamin D, calcium) [^e2b1ffa0].

---

## Practical recommendations

- **Individualized planning**: Tailor intake to injury level, activity, age, sex, and comorbidities [^2edf40c2].
- **Multidisciplinary care**: Coordinate with dietitians, physicians, therapists, and caregivers [^8e44e1de].
- **Education**: Provide nutrition education on portion sizes, supplement use, and bowel management [^4286cf00].

Nutrition in chronic SCI requires **individualized, evidence-informed plans** that balance energy intake with reduced expenditure, prioritize protein and fiber, and address micronutrient deficits, with ongoing screening and multidisciplinary support to optimize health and function [^00782b25] [^c846d241].

### Investigation of measured and predicted resting energy needs in adults after spinal cord injury: a systematic review [^61067be7]. Spinal Cord (2016). Low credibility.

Background

Accurate estimation of energy needs is vital for effective nutritional management of individuals with spinal cord injury (SCI). Inappropriate energy prescription after SCI can compound the rates of malnutrition or obesity, increase the risk of complications and negatively influence outcomes. Energy requirements following SCI are not well understood, and there is currently no universally accepted method of estimating energy needs in clinical practice.

Study Design

This is a systematic literature review.

Objectives

The objectives of this study were to investigate and compare the measured resting energy needs of adults with SCI across different phases of rehabilitation, and to identify appropriate energy prediction equations for use in SCI.

Setting

This study was conducted in Australia.

Methods

MEDLINE, EMBASE and CENTRAL databases were searched for studies published between 1975 and April 2015, identifying 298 articles. Full articles in English language of adults with SCI who were fasted for a minimum of 8 hours before undergoing indirect calorimetry to measure resting energy expenditure (REE) for at least 20 min were selected. On the basis of the inclusion criteria, 18 articles remained for data extraction. One author extracted information from all articles, and inter-rater reliability was tested in five articles.

Results

REE across three phases of injury was assessed: acute, sub-acute and chronic. Few studies (n=2) have investigated REE in the acute and sub-acute injury stages of SCI recovery. The factors influencing chronic energy needs in SCI patient populations are many and varied, and a valid predictive equation for use in SCI remains elusive.

Conclusion

Indirect calorimetry remains the only accurate assessment of REE for health practitioners working with patients after SCI.

### Nutritional status in chronic spinal cord injury: a systematic review and meta-analysis [^00782b25]. Spinal Cord (2019). Medium credibility.

Study Design

Systematic review and meta-analysis.

Objectives

The objective was to investigate nutritional status in chronic spinal cord injury (SCI), and compare macronutrient and micronutrient intake to the recommended values by the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) 2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans.

Setting

United States of America.

Methods

A MEDLINE/PubMed, Google Scholar, Scopus, and Web of Science search was performed, identifying 268 papers. All papers included were English-language papers examining adults with chronic SCI. A meta-analysis was performed to produce weighted averages and 95% confidence intervals (CI) when summary statistics were provided.

Results

The systematic review included 15 articles, while the meta-analysis included 12. Resting metabolic rate (1492kcal/day; CI: 1414-1569) fell below the able-bodied average, and total energy (1876 kcal/day; CI: 1694-2059) and fiber (17g/day; CI: 14-20) intake were below USDA guidelines. Vitamins A, B5, B7, B9, D, E, potassium, and calcium were deficient, while vitamins B1, B2, B3, B12, C, K, sodium, phosphorus, copper, and zinc were in excess according to USDA guidelines. Vitamin B6, iron, and magnesium were within USDA guidelines.

Conclusion

Findings indicate greater energy intake relative to energy needs in those with chronic SCI, and an imbalance in fiber intake and micronutrients compared to the USDA guidelines. Future research examining nutritional health status is needed in order to establish evidence-based, SCI-specific dietary guidelines.

Introduction

A spinal cord injury (SCI) resulting from trauma or disease disrupts spinal cord pathways, leading to paralysis and adverse alterations in whole-body metabolism. The rapid onset of immobility and dysfunctional physiological health stemming from autonomic dysfunction, sublesional myopenia, imposed or adopted sedentary behaviour, and the overconsumption of energy relative to reduced total energy expenditure contributes to the development of neurogenic obesity. Pressure ulcers

Pressure ulcers, also known as bedsores, decubitus ulcers and pressure injuries, are localized areas of tissue necrosis developing when soft tissue is compressed between a bony prominence and an external surface for a prolonged period of time. They are an important problem in critically ill patients, older adults, and in persons with spinal cord injury and are one of the most common types of complex wound.

Pressure ulcers can offer an ideal environment for microbial colonization. This is especially true for those pressure ulcers that may be particularly exposed to Gram-negative and Gram-positive bacterial contamination from fecal material. However, all wounds can be colonized by micro‐organisms without leading to adverse events.

Standard care for adults with pressure ulcers includes a correct prevention and management.

Prevention of pressure ulcer formation is directed at alleviating the risk factors for the individual patient, and is primarily focused on minimizing episodes of prolonged pressure either by placing appropriate padding at pressure points or by frequent patient repositioning.

Debridement of devitalized tissue and biofilm and abscess drainage are necessary in the treatment of pressure ulcers. In cases where there is a significant amount of necrotic tissue, performing the initial debridement in the operating room allows for a more definitive procedure. Subsequent debridements are then more easily managed at the bedside.

Dressings should be chosen depending on the wound being treated. None of the dressings has been shown to have any superiority, and the choice of dressing should depend on the type of wound being treated.

Any open pressure ulcer is superficially contaminated with environmental flora. However, it is important to prevent added contamination if the wound is near the fecal stream as in ischial or sacral pressure ulcers. Systemic antibiotics should be administered only when there is clinical evidence of infection.
