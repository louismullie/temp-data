## Antibiotic duration trials

### BALANCE trial (2024)

- **Design**: Multicenter, open-label, randomized controlled trial (RCT).
- **Population**: Hospitalized patients with bloodstream infection (BSI).
- **Intervention**: 7-day antibiotic therapy vs. 14-day therapy.
- **Primary outcome**: 90-day all-cause mortality.
- **Results**: 7-day therapy was noninferior to 14-day therapy (14.5% vs. 16.1%; absolute risk difference -1.6%, 95% CI -4 to 0.8) [^115phhQJ].
- **Impact**: Supports shorter antibiotic courses for BSI, reducing exposure and resistance risk.

---

### APPIC trial (2023)

This multicenter, open-label, noninferiority RCT in patients with complex appendicitis compared 2 vs 5 days of postoperative antibiotics and assessed infectious complications and death within 90 days. The 2-day course was **noninferior** to 5 days (10% vs. 8%; absolute difference 2%, 95% CI -1.6 to 5.6) [^1177hEAT]. These findings support **shorter postoperative antibiotic courses** to reduce adverse effects and resistance.

### Progress in the fight against multidrug-resistant bacteria 2005–2016: Modern noninferiority trial designs enable antibiotic development in advance of epidemic bacterial resistance [^112eBS6o]. Clinical Infectious Diseases (2017). High credibility.

In a noninferiority trial, active treatments are compared with the knowledge that sufficiently similar response rates will permit a conclusion that clinically relevant differences in efficacy are very unlikely. Critical design elements for robust noninferiority trials include:

1. Disease definitions that identify patients with an acute, severe infection that requires antibiotic therapy.
2. A reliable and reproducible endpoint.
3. Data showing a benefit of active antibiotic therapy over placebo.

Recently, these elements have been refined for six well-characterized acute, severe bacterial infections: acute bacterial skin and skin structure infection, community-acquired bacterial pneumonia, hospital-acquired bacterial pneumonia, ventilator-associated bacterial pneumonia, complicated intra-abdominal infection, and complicated urinary tract infection.

The focus on these specific infections is an important evidence-based element underpinning the use of noninferiority designs for new agent registration. Although some have noted that, for example, a skin infection is not the most important potential use for a new antistaphylococcal agent, or that inclusion of outpatients suggests that patients lacked a life-threatening infection, such arguments fail to recognize that:

1. Patients meeting the definitions of new regulatory guidance have a severe infection that will have a poor outcome without efficacious antibiotic therapy.
2. Initial registration based on these criteria ensures effective new treatments are made available.
