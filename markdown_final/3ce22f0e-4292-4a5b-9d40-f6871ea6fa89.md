The lumbar muscles most commonly injected with trigger point injections are the **quadratus lumborum**, **erector spinae group** (iliocostalis lumborum, longissimus thoracis, spinalis), and **multifidus**. These muscles are frequent sources of myofascial pain and trigger points in the lumbar region, and injections are typically considered when conservative measures fail [^114jMmYN] [^1122cNTp].

## Quadratus lumborum (QL)

- **Anatomy and function**: The QL is a deep lumbar muscle that stabilizes the spine and pelvis, and assists with lateral flexion and extension [^113jRhUV].

- **Clinical relevance**: The QL is a common source of myofascial trigger points, often causing referred pain to the lower back, hip, and groin [^114jMmYN] [^116bcEQc].

- **Indications for injection**: Trigger point injections are indicated for persistent QL myofascial pain unresponsive to conservative treatments [^1166g83r].

## Erector spinae group

The erector spinae group comprises the iliocostalis lumborum, longissimus thoracis, and spinalis — **primary extensors and stabilizers** of the lumbar spine [^113chndq].

- **Iliocostalis lumborum**: Frequently involved in myofascial pain syndromes, with trigger points causing localized and referred pain [^114jMmYN] [^117APY8P].

- **Longissimus thoracis**: Commonly associated with lumbar myofascial pain, often requiring trigger point injections for relief [^116bcEQc].

- **Spinalis**: Less frequently targeted but may be involved in chronic lumbar pain syndromes.

## Multifidus

- **Anatomy and function**: The multifidus is a deep paraspinal muscle critical for segmental stability of the lumbar spine [^113Pvm9E] [^11599W2Y].

- **Clinical relevance**: Multifidus dysfunction and atrophy are strongly associated with chronic low back pain and lumbar instability [^113NdPZt] [^117AqVEg].

- **Indications for injection**: Trigger point injections are indicated for multifidus-related myofascial pain, particularly in chronic low back pain patients with documented muscle dysfunction [^1122cNTp].

## Clinical guidelines and evidence

The ACOEM 2021 guidelines recommend trigger point injections as a **second or tertiary option** for subacute or chronic low back pain that does not resolve with conservative measures [^1122cNTp]. The ASPN 2022 guidelines suggest trigger point injections with botulinum toxin type A in medically refractory cases [^113iYsYa]. Overall, trigger point injections can provide **short-term pain relief** and improved function in patients with lumbar myofascial pain syndromes, although **long-term efficacy remains uncertain** [^1166g83r] [^113UCFns].

### Trigger point management [^1166g83r]. American Family Physician (2023). Low credibility.

Trigger points producing myofascial pain syndromes are common in primary care. Located within skeletal muscle, these trigger points are taut, band-like nodules capable of producing pain and disability. Some evidence from clinical trials supports massage, physical therapy, and osteopathic manual medicine as first-line, less invasive treatment strategies.

Trigger points are often treated with injections. Although randomized trials have found statistically significant results with trigger point injections, conclusions are limited by low numbers of study participants, difficulty in blinding, the potential for a placebo effect, and lack of posttreatment follow-up. No single pharmacologic agent used in trigger point injections has been proven superior to another, nor has any single agent been proven superior to placebo. Therefore, trigger point injections should be reserved for patients whose myofascial pain has been refractory to other measures. Family physicians should first employ less invasive treatment strategies. Trigger point management is only one part of a comprehensive, multimodal, and team-based approach to patients with myofascial pain.

### Deep and superficial fibers of the lumbar multifidus muscle are differentially active during voluntary arm movements [^113Pvm9E]. Spine (2002). Low credibility.

This cross-sectional study was conducted to determine the activity of the deep and superficial fibers of the lumbar multifidus during voluntary movement of the arm. The multifidus contributes to the stability of the lumbar spine. The deep and superficial parts of the multifidus, located near the center of lumbar joint rotation, have distinct roles: the superficial fibers are well suited to control spine orientation, while the deep fibers are responsible for controlling intervertebral movement. However, there is a lack of in vivo data to support this distinction.

Electromyographic activity was recorded in the deep and superficial multifidus, transversus abdominis, erector spinae, and deltoid using selective intramuscular electrodes and surface electrodes during both single and repetitive arm movements. The latency of electromyographic onset in each muscle during single movements and the pattern of electromyographic activity during repetitive movements were compared between muscles.

With single arm movements, the onset of electromyography in the erector spinae and superficial multifidus relative to the deltoid depended on the direction of movement, whereas the onset in the deep multifidus and transversus abdominis did not. In repetitive arm movements, peaks in superficial multifidus and erector spinae electromyography occurred only during flexion for most subjects. In contrast, peaks in deep multifidus electromyography occurred during movement in both directions.

The conclusion of this study indicates that the deep and superficial fibers of the multifidus are indeed differentially active during voluntary arm movements.

### No quadriceps muscle weakness following anterior quadratus lumborum block compared with placebo: A randomized, non-inferiority, blinded, volunteer trial [^116PPYCK]. Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine (2025). High credibility.

This study explored the potential impact of an anterior quadratus lumborum block on quadriceps muscle strength, as this type of block is applied near the lumbar plexus and could lead to lower limb weakness. The objective was to determine whether a unilateral anterior quadratus lumborum block would result in quadriceps muscle weakness compared to a placebo.

- **Methods**: In this randomized, non-inferiority, triple-blind trial, 20 healthy volunteers received an active unilateral anterior quadratus lumborum block with 30 mL ropivacaine 0.75% on one side and a placebo block on the opposite side. The primary outcome measured was the change in maximal quadriceps muscle strength from baseline to 60 minutes post-block compared to placebo. Secondary outcomes included the change in results of the single-leg 6 m timed hop test, the Timed-Up and Go test, and mean arterial pressure from baseline to 30 minutes post-block, as well as dermatomal affection.

- **Results**: The trial found no statistically significant difference in changes in maximal quadriceps muscle strength between the active and placebo blocks, with a difference of 15.88 N (95% CI -12.19 to +43.94), p non-inf = 0.003, supporting non-inferiority. The Timed-Up and Go test was completed significantly faster at 60 minutes post-block; -0.23 seconds (95% CI -0.38 to -0.08, p = 0.005). The mean change in mean arterial pressure from baseline to 30 minutes post-block was 4.25 mm Hg (95% CI 0.24 to 8.26, p = 0.04). Dermatome testing indicated a primary affection of the lower abdomen (Th10-L1) with the active block.

- **Conclusion**: In this randomized controlled trial with healthy volunteers, a unilateral anterior quadratus lumborum block did not cause a statistically or clinically significant motor block of the quadriceps muscle.

### Consensus practice guidelines on interventions for lumbar facet joint pain from a multispecialty, international working group [^1123LdXC]. Regional Anesthesia and Pain Medicine (2020). Low credibility.

RFA-related degeneration of spinal anatomy and musculature

The multifidus muscle, the most medial of the deep intrinsic spinal muscles, contributes to segmental stability in the lumbar spine. RFA of the medial branches innervating the lumbar facet joints results in denervation of this muscle, but the physiological implications of this phenomenon are unclear. Dreyfuss et al. conducted a study on five patients who had undergone unilateral lumbar RFA. MRI of the lumbar spine was performed 17–26 months after denervation. Diffuse atrophy of the lumbar multifidus was noted in all patients, but was not limited to the levels of RFA even though post-RFA electromyography indicated denervation of multifidus muscles only at the levels where RFA was performed. None of the patients had recurrence of pain or evidence of spinal instability. In a larger case series of 27 patients, MRI was done prior to and following lumbar RFA to assess the bulk of the multifidus, and for evidence of disc and facet joint degeneration.
